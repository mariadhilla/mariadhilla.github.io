YelpMe

Reimagining how foodies plan meetups.

Overview

Role

Project Lead, UX/UI Designer, Prototype Developer

Time

3 months - 2022

Program

COGS 123: Social Computing @ UC San Diego

Deliverables

High fidelity clickable prototype and team case study

Background
Why does social eating matter?

We found that when people are eating, their moods are heavily affected by factors such as whether or not they are eating with others and who they are eating with. Additionally, social eating has actually been shown to build and reinforce relationships. Lastly, research has also shown that eating with others is correlated with better mental health and happiness.

Past research has shown that co-location is one of the strongest predictors of whether two people become friends because it affords “frequent, serendipitous interactions.” (Predicting Tie Strength With Social Media, Gilbert & Karahalios, 2009).

Problem
How might we streamline the planning of social eating to optimize connectivity between both weak and strong ties?

After spending 2 years being isolated from others because of COVID-19, it is harder than ever to coordinate hangouts. This leads to an increase in people eating alone. However, if we can use digital scheduling tools and integrate them into existing applications, it may make it easier for people to schedule in-person hangouts and form better connections with the people around them.


Why Yelp?

We came up with the idea to integrate an add-on to popular business-review app Yelp, specifically centered around restaurants. Although Yelp has a variety of information users can access, the platform offers no way for users to coordinate with one another with that information.

Our secondary research showed the benefits of social eating, but many don't participate in social eating due to the difficulty of planning/scheduling. We saw that 84.4% of our survey respondents use Yelp to find restaurants, but only 9.4% use Yelp to send recommendations to friends.

Solution

After completing two rounds of user testing, my teammates and I built screens on Figma to show how users can opt-in to YelpMe, our new add-on social feature. The screens demonstrate key design features that help facilitate social eating between friends and acquaintances:


Public/private pinning of restaurants

Through the use of public and private pins, we allow users to determine for themselves if they'd like to meet up with others at specific locations, or would rather keep their interests private.


Indicating interest in "matching" with other users

Once a user has publicly pinned a restaurant, they are able to see other friends and acquaintances who have also publicly pinned the same restaurant, and can choose to indicate interest in meeting up with them.


Being matched with friends & acquaintances

If and only if both users indicate a mutual interest in meeting with one another, they are prompted to message each other and potentially hang out.

Deep Dive

User Research
Surveys

We surveyed 32 users, taken from convenience sampling (primarily university students), to gauge their interest in restaurant/food-related decision-making, scheduling, and attitude.

Our findings:

  • Social eating is popular and beneficial
  • Co-location is difficult to work with when organizing
  • Yelp users use Yelp for information seeking, but there is a missed opportunity for social components
  • Half of our respondents (50%) did not wish to meet new people for social eating, and amongst that group, 63% said it is due to social discomfort

Challenges & Opportunities
  • Yelp has an edge in planning functionalities and coordination abilities, as well as a strong foundation for the discovery of food and restaurants in comparison to other platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, Pinterest, and Nextdoor
    • Opportunity to improve its current flow
  • The process of planning between friends can be more streamlined as all details and information about the restaurant is compiled within the app
    • Users do not have to perform their own research

Lo-Fi Prototype - Testing
User Flows

After getting to know our users better and determining our product goals, we created a user flow to help us guide our iterative process and explore our design space with a shared mental model.


For our user testing, we provided the same social setting for participants during our prototyping sessions:

"You are a Yelp user, and you want to check out new restaurants to eat at! You are also looking for a companion to eat with you. Whether this is a person you know very well or someone who you vaguely know…it is up to you!"


User Testing

During our first prototyping session, we used Google Forms to simulate our Yelp restaurant discovery/home page. The Google Form results were populated in a Google Sheet where our team utilized a “Wizard of Oz” technique to simulate the novel interaction of seamless event planning through Yelp.

Our participants comprised of two other teams in the class. Users in this prototyping session were told that they should act as though they were all "mutuals," meaning everyone had weak ties with each other already. However, their teammates were their close friends.


The backend on Google Sheet was performed synchronously and manually by our project team.

Google Form responses for two teams populated onto Google Sheets to help us determine who to group up based on the restaurant pinned. i.e. To Ellie Kanda: Sophia has just pinned the same restaurant (Red Panda Express)! Set up a meeting time?

Based on each user's response on the form, they would receive one of the following messages from us via private Zoom Messages:

“[Name] has just pinned the same restaurant! Set up a meeting time?”

“[Name], [Name], … have just pinned the same restaurant! Set up a meeting time?”


The user would not receive a message if there was no mutual/teammate that selected the same restaurants and meeting preferences as them. Based on the message(s) they received, they can determine whether they want to reach out first and make a “plan” to meet at their preferred restaurant via Zoom Messages.

We informed participants that an incentivizing feature to use YelpMe would be that they will get a discount at participating restaurants.

Hi-Fi Prototype - Testing

After considering user feedback from the previous prototyping session, we developed a high fidelity prototype of YelpMe. This time, the experience was entirely automated.

As I had the most coding experience in the team, I single-handedly coded this prototype to be as close as possible to a regular clickable prototype while limited by the capabilities of Google Sheets.

The prototype can be used in 4 simple steps!

  • You pin restaurants you're interested in. You can choose to privately pin the restaurant as well so it won't be made public to your connections.
  • If one of your connections is interested in the same restaurant, their name should appear in one of the “Friends Interested:” cells. If you want to meet with an interested friend (keep in mind whether they are a close friend or not), you can check the checkbox next to their name.
  • If they also checked the checkbox next to your name, you will see a text box that indicates that they also want to meet with you at the mutually pinned restaurant, at the very bottom of the Google Sheet.
  • You are now able to send each other messages to schedule this meetup!

You have publicly pinned Burger Queen, and can see that Samantha and Yvonne also pinned Burger Queen. You're only interested in meeting with Yvonne, so you check her name. Yvonne must also be interested in going with you to Burger Queen, because at the bottom of the file it generates a statement.


Check out an example prototype here!


For this prototype, we wanted to give users more control over who they would meet up with, rather than having us manually and randomly choose a person for them to eat with. We wanted to make sure both parties were mutually interested in meeting up with each other to eat. People are only notified that someone is interested in meeting with them if they are also interested; that way users can avoid awkward situations.

Additionally, we implemented a "Privately Pin Restaurant” feature for users who do not want to share certain restaurants they're interested in.

I used multiple matrices to keep track of which restaurants users indicated an interest in and to calculate when two users indicated mutual interest in each other. Using this information, we could notify users in real time when others matched with them.

This matrix keeps track of what restaurants users are interested in and sends this information back to users' screens by showing other interested users' names under the restaurant.

In this matrix depicting users' interest in going with each other to Limegrass, Samantha and Angela both indicated an interest in each other, so they would both get a notification.

Hi-Fi Prototype - Feedback

With our new high-fidelity prototype, we conducted user testing again with the same group of participants. We split the session into two groups, and each group had a total of 10 participants.

The first group used the prototype simultaneously, whereas the second group used the prototype in increments: 3 participants would use the prototype at the same time for 2 minutes, then the next 3 participants would use it for 2 minutes, then the final 4 participants used it.

The difference in prototyping methods was meant to highlight the different experiences users would face based on their timely interactions with other users. Realistically, usage of YelpMe would align more so with the incremental experience as users would most likely use the application at different times.

“It is an easy, lower-stakes/expectation on meeting people you kind of know.”

“I think it is an interesting idea since it shows you who is interested to go to which eating place with you. I would use it with weaker ties too to learn more about someone.”

“For weak ties, I may be more inclined to use it because I feel like there are lower stakes involved. We both kind of know each other and we can decide if we feel willing to just try and hang out.”


Many user's liked the low stakes aspect of interaction on YelpMe, and indicated a great interest in using something like YelpMe for getting to know their weaker ties better, as opposed to close friends. All users indicated that this design was novel.

I'm also honored to share that our hi-fi prototype was chosen by the professor to be shown as an example to future students in COGS 123!


Conclusions

Social Eating is Polarizing

Users responded in a variety of ways to our prototype:

“I'm the type of person who prefers eating alone than eating with weak ties... I feel a little bit awkward when eating with unfamiliar [people.]”

“I feel like we can use it for strangers as well.”


As we can surmise, some users were uncomfortable with the idea of eating with their weak ties, while others were fully on-board with expanding the reach of YelpMe to include complete strangers. As we went into our final prototype, we wanted to make sure that YelpMe remained a comfortable experience for all kinds of users.

Private and Public Pinning: The Next Novel Social Feature?

“[Flourish] out the private pin next time. Great second round of prototyping!”

“Nice prototype. I appreciate having a private pin as an option.”


From our user testing, one notable finding was that pinning privately is a feature that might be useful elsewhere. It may be a hidden feature that has intriguing implications in online social settings, as it creates a difference between pinning restaurants for yourself vs. others. It would be interesting to study when and why people would be more inclined to privately pin a restaurant over a public pin.

Next Steps

Once we completed our prototypes, we had a clear idea of how this extension could expand in the future:

  • Expand the meeting from 1:1 to group settings.
  • Embed calendar integration (Google Calendar/Microsoft Outlook)
  • Extend to non-restaurants (ie. various activities and locations also reviewed on Yelp)
